Sprint 1 Week 2

5 Whys Problem Analysis

Move from surface-level desires to root causes

SDL IS

Purpose

The 5 Whys technique helps you move from your initial understanding of the problem to its root cause. Often what we think is the problem is actually a symptom of something deeper.

Goal: Discover why you've been stuck. The answer to "Why #5" should reveal something you didn't fully recognize when you started.

The 5 Whys Chain

Start with your Superagency Challenge, then ask "Why?" five times. Each answer becomes the basis for the next question.

0
Starting Point
What is the problem or challenge you want to solve?
↓
1
Why #1
Why is this a problem? Why does it matter?
↓
2
Why #2
Why is THAT the case? What causes that?
↓
3
Why #3
Why does that happen? What's behind it?
↓
4
Why #4
Why? Go deeper.
↓
5
Why #5 — Root Cause
Why is that? What's at the core?

Example

Challenge: "I can't maintain a consistent exercise routine"

Why #1: I keep skipping workouts → Because I'm tired after work
Why #2: Why am I tired? → Because I don't sleep well
Why #3: Why don't I sleep well? → Because I'm anxious at night
Why #4: Why am I anxious? → Because I feel behind on work
Why #5: Why do I feel behind? → Because I don't have clear boundaries on when work ends
Root Cause: The exercise problem is actually a work-boundary problem. Solving exercise without addressing boundaries will fail.

What to Submit

  1. Your 5 Whys chain — Each step with your answer
  2. Your root cause — What did you discover at the bottom?
  3. The gap — How is the root cause different from what you initially thought the problem was?
  4. Draft problem statement — A clear statement of the real problem you'll solve

Success Criteria

Indicator High Medium Low
5 Whys Progression Each level reveals new insight; reaches a root cause that reframes the problem Logical progression but stops at intermediate cause Circular reasoning, superficial answers, or forced connections
Gap Recognition Clearly names difference between initial desire and discovered root barrier Senses a gap but can't articulate it precisely Root cause is same as initial complaint, just rephrased
Problem Statement Specific enough to evaluate whether a solution addresses it Directionally correct but too broad to guide design Vague, solution-embedded, or unchanged from Week 1
