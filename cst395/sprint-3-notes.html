    Covers: how the touchpoint analysis reveals two failure modes — “I know but I forget” vs. “I don’t know and can’t look it up” — and why that leads to two different product hypotheses.

    Framing informed by Tim Harford's Adapt — complex problems in unfamiliar domains can't be solved by planning, they have to be solved by experimenting.

    Key Points / Takeaways

    • Slow down. I am slowing down, not trying to get to a solution fast, but trying to get the direction right by asking more first-principle questions. Handwritten reflections are helping me to slow down.
    • Know yourself. I am trying to tap into my own understanding of myself and my experience. I am trying to hold myself to a high standard of recognizing what I don’t know and what mistakes I might make.
    • Iterate and take ownership. I am willing to go back and make quick adjustments (iterating and taking ownership/lead), instead of waiting for someone else to tell me what to do next.
    Notice what I did NOT do: I did not try to solve anything yet. I spent the class session understanding the document's structure, its assumptions, and its logic. The Context phase is about building a mental model of the problem space before you touch solutions.
