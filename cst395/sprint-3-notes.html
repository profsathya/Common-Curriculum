    Key Points / Takeaways

    • Slow down. I am slowing down, not trying to get to a solution fast, but trying to get the direction right by asking more first-principle questions. Handwritten reflections are helping me to slow down.
    • Know yourself. I am trying to tap into my own understanding of myself and my experience. I am trying to hold myself to a high standard of recognizing what I don’t know and what mistakes I might make.
    • Iterate and take ownership. I am willing to go back and make quick adjustments (iterating and taking ownership/lead), instead of waiting for someone else to tell me what to do next.
    Notice what I did NOT do: I did not try to solve anything yet. I spent the class session understanding the document's structure, its assumptions, and its logic. The Context phase is about building a mental model of the problem space before you touch solutions.

    Handwritten Reflection

    Planning: From Stake to Human Process

    Open Handwritten PDF →

    Covers: what Piranavan changed about my assumptions, picking a specific person, and designing the human process before touching technology.

    Takeaways

    • Write before you prompt. Your thinking shapes the AI session — not the other way around. I wrote ~200 words of my own direction before AI did anything. The push/pull hypothesis, the resources angle, the “high impact with small correction” framing — all of that came from reflection, not from a chat window.
    • Ask for options, not answers. “Give me three options with pros and cons” — not “tell me the best one.” I kept the decision with myself. That’s the difference between using AI as a tool and outsourcing your judgment to it.
    • Read everything with pen in hand. I required actual links so I could check sources myself. Then I read the entire response and took notes. If you skip this step, you’re building on a foundation you haven’t verified.
    More from this session
    • Load the session with everything you already have — I attached the guideline, the JSX artifact from the Map phase, copy-pasted the assignment framing, and added my push/pull hypothesis. Don’t start a cold chat.
    • UMPIRE phases aren’t a rigid sequence — I was in Plan but found myself doing Review and Evaluate on what AI produced. That’s normal. The framework is a thinking tool, not a checklist.
    • Other people’s perspectives change your thinking — talking to students surfaced the resources angle I hadn’t considered. More conversations = more inputs = better plan.
    • Catch yourself jumping to solutions too early — I noticed myself sketching color-coded paper clips and screen notices, then pulled back. Noticing that impulse is the skill.
    • This takes real time — 30 to 45 minutes per phase, not 10. The video shows the setup, not the full work.

    Handwritten Reflection

    From Plan to Implementation

    Open Handwritten PDF →

    Covers: what changed between the plan and the build, how expert feedback reshaped the approach, and the gap between intention and execution.

    Takeaways

    • Start with your own ideas, then let AI break them. I came in with a specific plan — green/yellow/red patient categorization, wristbands, color-coded clips on paperwork, QR codes to a web app. I didn’t ask AI to generate ideas. I asked it to evaluate mine. It found flaws I hadn’t considered. That’s the value.
    • AI caught what my engineering mindset missed. Five red patients but only one isolation room — what happens? Color-coded wristbands conflicting with existing hospital color systems. Patients panicking when they see a red tag. Family members who need guidance too. I was solving a technical problem. The AI saw the human problem.
    • The tool doesn’t matter — the approach does. I switched from ChatGPT to Gemini deliberately. Same method: load context, share your thinking, ask for challenge. The AI is interchangeable. Your process isn’t.
    More from this session
    • Load your context every time you switch tools — I added the guideline, the JSX artifact from Map, and my handwritten notes into a new Gemini gem before writing a single prompt
    • Ask “do you think this is feasible?” not “build this” — that invites pushback instead of compliance
    • Low-tech sometimes beats high-tech — a decision tree printed on the back of an intake form might work better than a web app with a QR code
    • Your earlier UMPIRE work should show up in your plan — I had thought about resource constraints during Map but completely dropped them when I started planning the solution. AI caught the gap I created
    • There is always more work than you think — the video is 12 minutes, the actual work continues off-camera

    Your Turn

    Your first build iteration is your version of this. You’ve done the Dojo, you’ve sharpened your stake, you’ve talked to Piranavan. Now take your plan and build something — then ask AI to break it before you show it to anyone. The test: did AI find a flaw you hadn’t considered? If not, you probably didn’t give it enough of your actual thinking to work with.
