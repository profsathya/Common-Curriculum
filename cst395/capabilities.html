The Three Capabilities

What you're developing throughout the course

CST395 is designed around three core capabilities that together enable you to create value in an AI-augmented world. These aren't separate skills—they work together. Every sprint activity develops multiple capabilities, with different emphases at different stages of your journey.

SDL

Self-Directed Learning

The ability to learn what you need, when you need it

What It Means

In a world where information is abundant and AI can retrieve facts instantly, the critical skill isn't knowing things—it's knowing how to learn what you need to solve the problem in front of you. Self-directed learning means you can identify knowledge gaps, fill them efficiently, and know when you've learned enough to act.

How It Shows Up in the Course

  • Productive Reflections — Structured thinking before AI interaction
  • Domain Learning — Rapidly understanding unfamiliar territory (Sprint 2-4)
  • 5 Whys Analysis — Going deeper to find root causes
  • Build Logs — Documenting what you're learning as you work
  • Bridge Reflections — Synthesizing learning between sprints

Growth Trajectory

Sprint 1: Learn about yourself and your own challenges
Sprint 2: Learn about someone else's domain
Sprint 3: Learn from limited information and indirect sources
Sprint 4: Independently identify what you need to learn

IS

Interpersonal Skills

The ability to understand and communicate with people

What It Means

AI can process information, but it can't truly understand people. The ability to listen, empathize, elicit real needs, and communicate value is fundamentally human. Interpersonal skills mean you can work effectively with stakeholders, collaborate with peers, and translate between technical and non-technical perspectives.

How It Shows Up in the Course

  • Peer Conversations — Weekly structured practice with a partner
  • Stakeholder Interviews — Learning to ask the right questions
  • Presentations — Communicating value, not features
  • Human Value Statements — Articulating your unique contribution
  • Proxy Validation — Getting feedback when direct access is limited

Growth Trajectory

Sprint 1: Understanding yourself as stakeholder
Sprint 2: Deep engagement with someone you know
Sprint 3: Building understanding without easy access
Sprint 4: Choosing and validating stakeholders independently

Peer Conversation Skills

Each week, you practice a specific interpersonal skill with your partner:

  • Week 1: Creating conditions for understanding
  • Week 2: Understanding reasoning processes
  • Week 3: Understanding decisions
  • Week 4: Drawing out self-understanding
  • Week 5: Challenging assumptions constructively
  • Week 6: Probing for depth of understanding
  • Week 7: Giving useful (not just polite) feedback
  • Week 8: Helping someone articulate growth
  • ...and more through Sprints 3 & 4
AB

AI-Augmented Building

The ability to create solutions with AI as a cognitive partner

What It Means

AI-augmented building isn't about using AI to code for you. It's about maintaining your judgment and direction while leveraging AI to extend your capabilities. You bring the "why" and the taste; AI helps with the "how." This means knowing when to use AI, how to guide it effectively, and how to evaluate and improve its outputs.

How It Shows Up in the Course

  • UMPIRE Framework — Structured approach to problem-solving
  • 3Cs (Context-Choices-Confirmation) — Effective AI interactions
  • Solution Architecture — Design before implementation
  • Build Logs — Documenting human decisions in the process
  • Human Value Statements — Identifying what YOU contributed

Growth Trajectory

Sprint 1: Build with high guidance and structured frameworks
Sprint 2: Build for someone else's needs, adapting to feedback
Sprint 3: Build with constraints and limited information
Sprint 4: Build with full autonomy over process and approach

The Human Value Question

Every sprint ends with a Human Value Statement where you articulate what YOU specifically contributed that AI couldn't. This isn't about competing with AI—it's about understanding where human judgment, taste, and direction are essential. Examples include:

  • Deciding what problem was worth solving
  • Understanding the nuances of stakeholder needs
  • Making tradeoffs between competing priorities
  • Knowing when "good enough" was actually good enough
  • Recognizing when the technical solution missed the human point

How They Work Together

These capabilities aren't separate—they're integrated:

  • SDL + IS: Learning about people and their needs requires both self-directed inquiry and interpersonal engagement
  • IS + AB: Building for others requires understanding their needs and translating that into solutions
  • AB + SDL: Building effectively requires continuously learning new domains and techniques
  • All three: Creating real value requires learning what's needed, understanding who needs it, and building something that delivers
