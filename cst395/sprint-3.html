Sprint 3: Complexity

"Real Stakes" — Solving for people whose lives depend on getting it right

Weeks 9-12 External Stakeholder Concept: Human Value

🌍 The Real Problem

The Ministry of Health of Bhutan has developed a national guideline for managing Multidrug-Resistant Organisms (MDRO) — bacteria that resist most available antibiotics. Globally, 1.27 million people die every year from antibiotic-resistant infections. In South Asia, the rate is 76.8 deaths per 100,000 population.

The guideline exists. The problem: it's a dense 28-page clinical document. Physicians in a busy ER, nurses managing isolation protocols, cleaning staff following disinfection procedures — none of them can pause to read and interpret it in the moment they need it.

Piranavan Selvanandan, a technical consultant, was approached by contacts in Bhutan to help make this guideline actionable. He's bringing that challenge to you. He'll be available for two class sessions and by email/Slack throughout the sprint.

Sprint 3 Outcome

Identify one specific, underserved need within this clinical guideline, build a solution that genuinely addresses it for a named user in a specific context, and demonstrate that your solution reflects domain understanding — not just technical execution.

By Sprint End, You Will Have:

  • Entered an unfamiliar, high-stakes domain (clinical healthcare in a resource-limited setting)
  • Identified a problem worth solving — using AI to help you discover it, not to decide it for you
  • Built a solution with genuine human relevance: the right user, the right context, the right constraints
  • Engaged with an external technical stakeholder and (potentially) clinical professionals
  • Demonstrated that your process — not just your output — is reliable enough to trust
Sprint 3 Grading: Goals 20% (individual) · Reflections 40% (individual) · Demo 40% (individual)
🚦 The Differentiation Rule: No two students may work on problems that are more than 50% similar. In Week 9, you'll pitch your candidate problem in one sentence. The class decides if there's a collision. If there is, both students have 48 hours to differentiate their framing. First to stake a clear, specific claim owns the territory.

👥 Peer Conversation (In-Class + After)

Problem Pitch + Honest Pushback

"What problem are you solving? Who specifically is your user — not a general role, a specific person in a specific moment? What's the one thing that would make your solution useless to them?"

Skill: Asking questions that reveal assumptions the speaker hasn't noticed yet — without solving the problem for them.

Concepts: Building domain knowledge from primary sources. Using expert access strategically — you only get a few hours; spend them on what you can't learn from a document.
