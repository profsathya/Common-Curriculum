Sprint 3: Complexity

"Build narrow. Test fast. Expand." — Solving for a real person in a domain you have to learn

Weeks 9–12 External Stakeholder Concepts: Human Value, Abstraction

The Ministry of Health of Bhutan has a 28-page clinical guideline for managing multidrug-resistant organisms — bacteria that resist most antibiotics. Globally, antibiotic resistance kills 1.27 million people per year. The guideline exists. The problem: no one working in a busy ER, an isolation ward, or a cleaning role can pause to read and interpret it in the moment they need it.

Piranavan Selvanandan, a technical consultant approached by contacts in Bhutan to make this guideline actionable, is bringing this challenge directly to you. He'll work with you in Weeks 10 and 11.

Your job is not to solve the whole problem. Find one specific human moment where the guideline fails the person who needs it. Design a human process that addresses that moment. Build something that supports that process. Iterate.

Peer Interviews + Expert Access
Interview Stations (Prof. Sathya + Jeremy): Pair up with a partner. Student A presents their stake (~60–90 sec). Student B listens, then writes 2–3 questions in their notebook (60 sec). Student B shares their questions — you'll get feedback on the questions, not the stake. Are they probing assumptions? Or just asking for more detail? Swap roles and repeat. ~15 min per pair.
Piranavan (Zoom): After your peer interview, go 1-on-1 with Piranavan. Present your stake, ask specific questions, or both. ~5 min each. He can speak to clinical workflow, tech infrastructure in Bhutan, deployment realism, staffing, device access — not limited to medical topics. Not everyone will reach him during class — overflow students sign up for a next-day appointment.

Practice Interview + Build

Run the full peer interview with your partner using the sharpened questions. Then start designing the human process — touch the computer after you've designed the process on paper.

Key principle: We're evaluating question quality, not your stake. A partner who asks “how do you know the nurse does that?” is more useful than one who says “that sounds great.”
