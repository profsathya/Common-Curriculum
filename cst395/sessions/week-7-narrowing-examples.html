The Narrowing Move — See How It Works

Pick the scenario closest to your partner's problem. You'll step through a Dojo conversation — but at each step, you answer a question about the thinking before you see what happens next. This is exactly how your own session will work.

Scenario A
Task Delay
Pushes work off all week, mentally estimates how late he can start, calendars don't stick
Scenario B
Overcommitment
Too many clubs, wants sleep + interview prep, can't say no to social pressure
Scenario C
Job Search
Wants LeetCode + job apps but browses listings without ever applying
Round 1 — State What You Know
Student
My partner is a CS student who procrastinates. His hardest day is when he has to do everything he pushed off. He mentally estimates how long tasks take and uses that to justify delaying. He said "I've tried to create a calendar to section off my time" but it failed because "there's no real push for accountability." I think I need to build something that pushes him to start earlier.
Before you see the AI's response
The student says "I need to build something that pushes him to start earlier." What's the problem with jumping to that conclusion?
It's a solution before understanding the specific problem
The partner already tried calendars — "pushing" is the same idea
It assumes "starting earlier" is the right goal — maybe it's not about timing
All of the above
All of them. The student jumped to a solution type ("push to start earlier") based on a surface reading. The AI's job in Round 1 is to separate what the student observed from what they assumed. Watch how it redirects — it doesn't give answers, it asks what happens in the specific moment of decision.
Dojo AI
"Pushes him to start earlier" — that's already a solution assumption. What if starting earlier isn't the right target? When he scans an assignment and decides to defer, what happens right after? Does he feel relief? Anxiety? Does he write the estimate down?
Student
He said he feels fine in the moment — like a rational decision. The bad feeling comes later when it piles up. He doesn't write anything down, it's all in his head.
Dojo AI
So there's a gap: the estimation feels rational in the moment, but creates anxiety all week. He knows the pattern. Awareness isn't the problem. You need the minute-by-minute — not the general story.

⏸ PARTNER PAUSE #1

"Walk me through yesterday — when you got an assignment, what happened in the first 10 minutes? Step by step."

Think about this
Why does the AI insist on "yesterday, minute by minute" instead of accepting the general pattern?
General patterns hide the intervention point. "He procrastinates" is a label. "He opened Canvas, skimmed for 30 seconds, thought 'I can do this Thursday,' and closed the tab in under a minute" — that's a sequence with specific moments you can design for. You can't build for "procrastination." You can build for "the 30 seconds between skimming and closing Canvas."
Round 2 — Find the Sub-Problems
What the partner said
"Yesterday I got a Canvas notification, opened it on my phone, skimmed the requirements for about 30 seconds, thought 'this is maybe 2 hours, I could do it Thursday night.' Closed Canvas. The whole thing took less than a minute. Didn't think about it again until Wednesday when two more had stacked up."
Dojo AI
Three distinct sub-problems:
1. The 30-second skim: Reads just enough to estimate, not to understand. Estimate may be wrong.
2. The "future self" handoff: Delegates to Thursday-him, assuming that version will have energy and no new work.
3. Invisible accumulation: Nothing tracks what he deferred. By Wednesday, three things piled up.

Before you pick — research says this isn't a discipline problem. Procrastination researchers found it's emotion regulation. People delay tasks that trigger negative emotions, and the "future self" estimate is systematically biased — we imagine a version more motivated than we'll actually be. This is also temporal discounting: the further away a deadline, the less real it feels.

This means a reminder or "push" system won't work. He'll ignore it like the calendar.

⏸ PARTNER PAUSE #2

"Of those three — the quick skim, handing it off to future-you, or not tracking what you've deferred — which one, if it disappeared, would change your week the most?"

Your turn to narrow
Of the three sub-problems — which one do you think is the trigger? The first domino? And why?
The partner is the tiebreaker — not you, not the AI. You might have a theory. But only the partner knows their experience. Watch — he picks the one the student didn't expect.
Round 3 — Define What to Build
Partner's answer
"The future-self thing. The skimming doesn't bother me, and I can see what's due on Canvas. The problem is I keep telling myself I'll do it later and I always believe myself, even though I'm always wrong about it."
Dojo AI
He's aware and still does it. So the intervention isn't awareness — it's friction between the decision and the action. Something that makes the "future self" handoff harder to do on autopilot. Don't default to "app." Think about how he actually operates — he skims on his phone, closes Canvas in under a minute.
Student
What if he had to write down his commitment — task, when he'll do it, how long? So he can see the pile building.
Dojo AI
That's designing for accumulation, but he said the handoff is the trigger. What if the tool forced a question: "You're telling future-you to do this Thursday. What's future-you giving up to make room?" That's the question he never asks. "Thursday" feels free. It's not.

⏸ PARTNER PAUSE #3

"Would you actually use something that makes you pause and answer 'what is Thursday-you giving up?' — or would you skip it?"

Design instinct check
The AI redirected from "make the pile visible" to "make the deferral harder." Why is that a better target?
Visibility is after the fact — friction is at the moment of decision
He already sees the pile on Canvas — another tracker won't change behavior
The partner said the handoff is the problem, so build for the handoff
All of these
All of them — and the partner is about to change the plan entirely. This is the most important moment: the partner's honest reaction to your idea matters more than the idea itself.
Partner's honest reaction
"A form or app? I'd probably skip it. But honestly — when you asked me that question just now, face to face, I actually stopped and thought about it. I wouldn't do that alone with a screen."
Catch the insight
The partner just told you what form the solution needs to take. What is it?
Not an app. A person. The partner said a human question works where a screen doesn't. The intervention is social, not technical. The student's CS instinct was software. The partner's experience said otherwise.
Round 4 — Locked MVP

Final MVP

Broad problem: Partner delays assignments all week, then crams.

Specific component: The "future self" handoff — deciding to defer without questioning the cost.

What I'm building: A daily 2-minute check-in via text. When he defers a task, he texts three things: the task, when he'll do it, and what that time was already claimed for. I respond: "Do you still want to make that trade?"

How he uses it: Once daily via text when he gets new assignments.

Measurable: Over 5 days, how many decisions change after check-in. If 2/5 change, something shifted.

Carry this into your session

Started thinking "productivity app." Ended with a text protocol. Three things made that happen: (1) the AI demanded minute-by-minute instead of the general story, (2) research on emotion regulation killed the "push harder" instinct, (3) the partner said "I'd think about it if someone asked me" — and that determined the form. Your partner will do the same if you ask the right questions.

They don't actually know which to drop
They know, but the social cost of dropping things feels too high
Being "involved" is part of their identity
They haven't felt consequences hard enough
Social cost and identity. They know sleep and interviews matter more. A prioritization system solves a problem they don't have. The AI tests this immediately.

Student
They said they "want to be part of a lot of things" and care about their commitments.
Dojo AI
Are they staying because the clubs matter, or because leaving feels like failing? You need the specific daily experience — not the general overload.

⏸ PARTNER PAUSE #1

"Pick one evening last week where you wanted to do interview prep or sleep early but couldn't. What happened instead?"

What to listen for
When the partner describes that evening, what specific moment should the student be looking for? Not the schedule — the decision point.
The moment they chose to stay instead of leave. Not "I had a meeting" (schedule fact) — but "the meeting ended and I stayed because..." That's where the intervention lives.
Round 2 — Find the Sub-Problems
Partner's answer
"Tuesday, club meeting 6-8pm. I'm an officer so I had to go. After, members wanted to hang out. Stayed until 10pm because I felt bad saying no. Got home, too late for interview prep. I know I should have left, but I didn't want to be that person."
Dojo AI
"I didn't want to be that person." Two sub-problems:
1. Role obligations: Officer status creates mandatory presence.
2. Boundary moments: After the meeting, leaving requires overriding live social pressure.

Research: this is commitment escalation — continuing because you've invested, not because it's valuable. Also identity-based decisions: "club officer" is who they are. A time-blocking tool won't work because at 8pm Tuesday, social pressure overrides any plan.

⏸ PARTNER PAUSE #2

"Two things: the officer role that makes you show up, and the pressure to stay late. Which one, if it disappeared, would give you the most time back?"

Anticipate the form
If the partner says the in-the-moment boundary is the problem (not the meetings themselves), what kind of solution does that point toward?
Social skills, not scheduling. "I can't leave when I want to" isn't fixed by any calendar or tracker. The solution gives them either a practiced exit method or an external reason that makes leaving socially OK.
Round 3 — Define What to Build
Partner
"The in-the-moment thing. I don't mind meetings. It's the after. If I could just leave when the meeting ends, I'd gain like 6 hours a week."
Student
Maybe prepared scripts — like "I've got something at 8, I need to head out"?
Dojo AI
Scripts alone won't work — they know they should leave. What if before each meeting, they pre-commit to an exit time by texting you? You text at that time. External accountability for the exit, not just the arrival.

⏸ PARTNER PAUSE #3

"What if before each meeting you text me when you plan to leave, and I text you? Would that help or would you ignore it?"

Partner's reaction
"If someone texted me I'd probably actually leave. It gives me an excuse — 'someone's expecting me.' The excuse matters more than the reminder."
Catch the design insight
"The excuse matters more than the reminder." How should that one sentence change what gets built?
The system's job isn't reminding — it's providing social cover. The text is evidence: "Someone needs me." Design for making the excuse real, not for notification features.
Round 4 — Locked MVP

Final MVP

Broad problem: Partner overcommitted, losing sleep + interview prep time.

Specific component: The post-meeting social tail — can't exit when the meeting ends.

What I'm building: Pre-commitment + exit protocol. Before each event, they text me exit time and what they're protecting. I text when time's up. It's both reminder and social excuse.

How they use it: Before each club event for one week (~3-4 events).

Measurable: Hours reclaimed after meetings. Currently ~6 hrs/week lost. Leave on time for 3/4 events = 4+ hours back.

Carry this into your session

Started with "prioritization system." Ended with "a text before each meeting." The partner's Tuesday story revealed the decision point was after meetings, not during. "The excuse matters more than the reminder" shaped the final design. Your partner's story will show you where the intervention actually lives.

Student

The student wants to build a "tracker." But the partner also said his time "isn't used best." If the real problem is how time gets used, will a tracker fix that? What assumption is being skipped?
The student is building what the partner asked for instead of what the partner needs. A tracker helps someone who's already applying. It doesn't help someone who hasn't started. The student needs to find out what happens when his partner tries to job search — and fails.
Dojo AI
Your partner listed five things — LeetCode, applications, tracker, CTI, MINT, micro-internships. That's a wishlist, not a problem. You jumped to "tracker" because he asked for it. But "time isn't used best" means tracking isn't the bottleneck. Something prevents him from using the time he has. What does he do instead of job stuff?

⏸ PARTNER PAUSE #1

"When was the last time you sat down to work on job stuff? Walk me through what happened from the moment you opened your laptop."

Why "nothing surprised me" matters
The AI flagged "nothing surprised me" as a problem. Why is surprise important in a discovery conversation?
If nothing surprises you, you only heard what you already believed
Your questions confirmed assumptions instead of challenging them
You didn't go deep enough to find the unexpected
All of the above
All of them. A good discovery should surprise you at least once. If everything matches your expectations, you're hearing the surface story. The specific failed attempt is where the real problem lives.
Round 2 — Find the Sub-Problems
Partner's story
"Had a free afternoon. Thought 'I should apply to jobs.' Opened LinkedIn, started browsing. Immediately overwhelmed — hundreds of results. Spent 20 minutes scrolling, opened a few listings, saw requirements I wasn't sure I met, eventually closed the tab. Ended up watching YouTube tutorials instead. Felt productive but wasn't job searching."
Dojo AI
Three sub-problems:
1. Overwhelm at entry: LinkedIn floods him with options he can't evaluate.
2. Qualification uncertainty: Can't tell if he qualifies. Every listing is a "maybe." Too many maybes = paralysis.
3. Productive-feeling substitute: YouTube tutorials feel like career development but avoid the actual task. This is productive procrastination.

Career research says the #1 barrier isn't motivation — it's role clarity. People who can't articulate what role they're seeking spend 3-4x longer browsing and apply less. Also: decision fatigue — after evaluating ~10 listings without applying, most people quit. Energy goes to evaluating, not applying.

A tracker doesn't help if he never gets past browsing.

⏸ PARTNER PAUSE #2

"Three things happened: too many options, not knowing if you qualify, and switching to YouTube. Which one — if it disappeared — would get you to actually submit an application?"

Research changes the target
The AI introduced "role clarity" and "decision fatigue." How does this research change what the student should build? What's the difference between what the partner asked for (tracker) and what the research reveals?
The bottleneck is before tracking. He never gets to the point where tracking helps. The research says he's stuck at evaluation — 20 minutes scrolling, 0 applications. The solution targets those 20 minutes, not the nonexistent application list.

Partner

"Two options: paste a listing into a tool, or a one-page cheat sheet you keep open while browsing. Which would you actually use next Tuesday?"

Predict the partner
High-tech (paste + analyze) vs. low-tech (cheat sheet in a tab). Which will the partner pick?
Paste tool — it's more accurate and does the work
Cheat sheet — lower friction, works inside the browsing flow
Doesn't matter — both work equally
The cheat sheet. Accuracy doesn't matter if the tool interrupts the flow. He's already on LinkedIn — the solution works inside that, not alongside it. Lower friction almost always wins.

Partner
"The cheat sheet. I don't want to copy-paste. If I could glance at something and know in 30 seconds whether to apply or skip, that would change everything. Especially if it's based on my resume."

Final MVP

Broad problem: Partner browses job listings without applying.

Specific component: Qualification uncertainty — can't quickly tell if a listing matches his level.

What I'm building: Personalized "Job Listing Cheat Sheet" — one page based on his resume and target roles. Keywords that signal entry-level vs. senior, his skills mapped to common requirements, apply/skip/stretch decision framework. Evaluates any listing in under 30 seconds.

How he uses it: Pinned tab next to LinkedIn. 3 dedicated search sessions this week.

Measurable: Listings evaluated per session (currently ~10, 0 apps) and applications submitted. Goal: 3+ applications across 3 sessions.
