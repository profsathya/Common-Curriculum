Pattern #1 ¬∑ From Your Submissions

Evidence vs.
Your Story About the Evidence

This showed up in almost every submission. You write "the evidence is..." and then state your interpretation.

What one student wrote as "evidence"

"He'd rather watch movies than go to the gym."

‚Üí Conclusion: "He needs to stop being lazy."

That's evidence he chooses rest sometimes. The word "lazy" is yours, not his. Preference ‚â† character flaw.

What another student wrote

"He said he doesn't like the taste of plain water and described it as 'bland.' He mentioned liking the taste and fizz of diet soda."

‚Üí Then admitted: "I'm assuming taste alone is the main barrier, but I don't yet have confirmation that caffeine or sweetness isn't also important."

Quotes what was said. Flags what was guessed. That's the difference.

In industry, a client will rarely correct your story about them. They just stop returning your calls.
Your partner today will tell you ‚Äî treat that as a gift.

Pattern #2 ¬∑ From Your Submissions

"If I'm Wrong, I'd
Change My Approach"

That was the most common answer to "what changes if this assumption is false?" It's not an answer. It's an escape hatch.

Escape hatch

"If this assumption is wrong, I would need to change my approach and focus on a different issue."

Change to what? Focus on which issue? This tells you nothing about what to do on Wednesday at 2pm.

Actual contingency

"If tradeoffs-first is wrong, the artifact changes from a keep/pause/reduce table to a weekly template with fixed commitments mapped first, then protected sleep and interview blocks."

Two different artifacts. Two different approaches. When the assumption breaks, you already know where to go.

Why this matters in 15 minutes

Your partner is going to tell you something you got wrong. If your only plan is "I'll change my approach" ‚Äî you'll freeze. If you've already thought about specifically what changes, you can pivot in real time.

This is what professionals do. They don't hope they're right. They prepare for being wrong.

Pattern #3 ¬∑ From Your Submissions

You Built a Solution
For Yourself

Several of you caught this. Most didn't. The ones who caught it are ahead.

From your own words:

"I have no evidence. I just used my experience with the general dislike of apps from the popular opinion online."

"I think that my solution to push him might be interpretation because that's what I needed to fix a similar issue."

Projecting your experience onto someone else is the most natural thing in the world.
It's also the fastest way to build something nobody uses.

These students aren't ahead because they avoided projection. They're ahead because they named it. You can't fix what you don't see.

During the challenge: if your partner says something that surprises you ‚Äî pay attention. That surprise is the distance between their reality and your projection.

Guided Demo ¬∑ 5 minutes

How the Challenge Works

The Move Set

  1. Read your assumption out loud ‚Äî exactly as written. Don't soften it. Don't add qualifiers.
  2. Tell your partner what you're basing it on: "You said [X]. I assumed [Y]."
  3. Ask directly: "Am I right, wrong, or is it more complicated?"
  4. Write down what they say. Not later. Now. On the same paper.
Example

"I assumed your main problem is that you eat when you're not hungry because of cravings. You told me you eat ahead of your meal prep sometimes. But I'm guessing the 'why' ‚Äî I don't actually know if it's cravings, boredom, or something else."

"Am I right about cravings, or is something else going on?"

What might come back

‚úÖ "Yeah, it's mostly cravings." ‚Üí Your assumption holds.

üîÑ "It's more that I'm bored when I'm studying." ‚Üí Close but different trigger. Your approach forks.

‚ùå "Actually the real thing is I cook too much and feel guilty wasting it." ‚Üí Completely different problem. Everything changes.

The goal is not to be right. The goal is to find where you're wrong before you build.

Challenge Round 1 ¬∑ You Present

Present Your Assumptions

Read each assumption. Let your partner respond. Write down what changes.

15:00

Presenter: Read your 3 assumptions exactly as written

  1. State the assumption
  2. Say what evidence you're basing it on vs. what you guessed
  3. Ask: "Am I right, wrong, or is it more complicated?"
  4. Write what your partner actually says ‚Äî on the same paper, right next to the assumption

Partner / Stakeholder: Your job

  • If they got it right ‚Äî say so, and say what specifically they understood
  • If they're close ‚Äî explain what's missing. Don't just say "not quite"
  • If they're wrong ‚Äî say it directly. In industry, clients don't tell you you're wrong ‚Äî they just stop calling. You're giving your partner something more valuable: honest correction.
  • If they projected their own experience onto you ‚Äî tell them: "That's your thing, not mine."

Second Half ¬∑ Demo Design

Sprint 2 Demo:
Showing Your Process

The demo isn't a feature tour. It's showing how you understood someone ‚Äî and what happened when you were wrong.

Demo Structure ‚Äî 5 Beats

  1. "Here's my partner and the problem I discovered."
    Not what you assumed. What you actually learned.
  2. "Here's what I assumed ‚Äî and where I was wrong."
    Today's challenge session gave you this. Use it.
  3. "Here's what I built and the key decisions I made."
    Why this approach and not a different one?
  4. "Here's what changed after the challenge."
    What did you revise, cut, or add based on what your partner told you?
  5. "Here's what would be worse if I'd just handed this to AI."
    What required your judgment, relationship, or understanding that a tool couldn't provide?

Present as if a hiring committee asked: "Show us how you solved a problem for someone."
The strongest demos show where you were wrong and what you did about it.
