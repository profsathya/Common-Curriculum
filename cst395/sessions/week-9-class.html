Where We're Going

Superagency and Human Value Proposition are not just skills.

"Superagency is an attitude: choosing to attempt things beyond your current capability and knowing how to close that gap. You can't practice it in an exercise. You can only create conditions where it either happens or it doesn't."

Why SDL + IS + AB

We're taking on something none of us have domain expertise in.

"None of us — not you, not me as an engineer, not me as a teacher, not even Piranavan — has the full expertise to navigate this confidently."

SDL — Self-Directed Learning

Nobody can tell you what you need to learn next in an unfamiliar clinical domain. You have to figure that out. That's what makes this real.

IS — Integrative Synthesis

Clinical context, technical constraints, human workflow, and the specific person in the moment don't come pre-integrated. Connecting them is the work.

AB — Adaptive Building

You can't wait until you fully understand the domain. You'll never get there that way. Build, test, adjust under genuine uncertainty.

When all three are working simultaneously — that's where Superagency lives. You own this. We're figuring it out together, and that's not a hedge. It's the actual condition.

Sprint 3 · The Challenge

A 28-page guideline that nobody can read in the moment they need it.

1.27 million deaths/year globally from antibiotic-resistant infections

Bhutan's Ministry of Health has a complete, well-researched MDRO clinical guideline

The problem: it's designed for policy review, not clinical action

Your job, to start with, is to find one specific human moment where the guideline fails the person who needs it. One moment.

Piranavan Selvanandan joins us in Weeks 10 and 11.

The Exercise · Why We're Doing This

We're going to learn about the domain together — by asking AI.

None of us knows what a clinical handoff looks like in a district hospital in Bhutan. The AI doesn't fully know either — but it knows more than we do right now, and it can search.

We're going to build a conversation together, live. I type. You tell me what to ask next.

The goal isn't one good answer. It's to understand the problem well enough to find where it breaks into smaller, ownable pieces.

Round 1 · Individual

Write the overall problem in one sentence.

This is not your narrow slice — you don't know enough yet to narrow. Write the big problem: what is broken for clinical workers because the MDRO guideline exists but isn't actionable in the moment they need it?

One sentence. Plain language. No jargon. If it could describe any country's healthcare system — rewrite it.

1. Write it in your notebook first

2. Then type it into your tab under "Round 1 · My Sentence"

5:00

Round 2 · Pairs

Did you describe the same problem?

Open your partner's tab. Read their sentence. Where does it say something different from yours — not better or worse, just different? Type your answer in your tab under "Round 2 · Where We Diverged."

That divergence is a question. It's what the class conversation will dig into.

5:00

Live · We Learn Together

We don't know the answers. Let's find out.

Step 1 — I read the sentences, you don't have to perform

I've been scanning the tabs while you worked. I'll name what I noticed — the words that kept appearing differently. That divergence is our first question.

Step 2 — First prompt, together

We compose an opening prompt — I type what we agree on. Web search enabled. We read the output together.

Step 3 — Follow-up questions from the room

You suggest what to ask next. I refine and type it. We're building a conversation, not reading a document.

Step 4 — The landscape emerges

After 25–30 minutes of this conversation, the big problem should have broken into smaller pieces. Some of those pieces are yours to own.

What we're trying to learn:

→ Who actually works in these settings and what does their day look like?

→ What happens in a clinical handoff in a resource-limited hospital?

→ What does "resource-limited" actually mean — no internet? Shared devices? Paper records?

→ What are the moments in the guideline where a worker most needs a fast answer?

From Landscape to Stake

The big problem just broke into smaller ones. Which one is yours to start with?

You now know more about this domain than you did 40 minutes ago. Not enough — but more. A territory isn't the final problem you'll solve. It's the specific piece of the landscape you're going to start from.

Not a territory

"Making the guideline more accessible"

"Helping clinical staff understand MDRO"

"Improving communication in hospitals"

A territory to start with

"Ward nurses during patient handoff — de-escalation decisions"

"Cleaning staff — which surfaces, which organisms, which protocol"

"ER physicians — identifying isolation need in the first 10 minutes"

This is your territory to start with. Not forever. To start.

Type your territory in the Google Doc under "My Territory." Write it before saying it out loud.

The Work Begins

Two questions. Your best current guesses.

1. What does your user probably do right now?

Based on what we just learned: what does your user probably do right now when they hit this moment?

2. What is the smallest thing that would make it better?

Not the ideal solution. The smallest meaningful improvement you can imagine right now.

You don't have the answers. Neither do I. Write your best current understanding and flag what's still a guess. That flagging is as important as the answer.

Type your answers in your tab under "First Design Sketch."
