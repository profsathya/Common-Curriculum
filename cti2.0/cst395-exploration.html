CST395 Exploration Guide

A live experiment in developing Superagency + Human Value

What You're Exploring

CST395: AI-Native Solution Engineering is running right now at CSUMB. It's not a theoretical framework—it's a live experiment in helping students develop Superagency and Human Value Proposition as a way of thinking.

Important: Canvas is used only for assignment submission. The actual learning experience happens through these interactive pages. This is fundamentally different from traditional course design.

As you explore: What here could inform what CTI offers? What works? What's unclear? What assumptions is this making?

Peer Conversations

▼

Perhaps the most unusual element—peer conversations are graded, tracked, and central.

Open Peer Conversations Page →

Look for:

  • The opening hook: "Who do you call when you're stuck?"
  • Four skills across sprints: Understanding → Challenging → Thought Partnership → Accountability
  • The "breadth + depth" model—why both matter
  • How it's tracked—both partners must submit for credit
  • The "5 years from now" reflection

Why This Matters

Integration (the "IS" capability) requires genuine relatedness—understanding problems from multiple perspectives through actual human interaction, not just cognitive synthesis. This is that in practice.

Questions to consider:

  • Will students actually do this? What would make them resist?
  • Is grading conversations problematic or necessary?
  • How does this relate to what CTI already values?

Sprint 1 Structure

▼

See what the student experience actually looks like week by week.

Open Sprint 1 Page →

Look for:

  • Sprint framing: "Superagency Over Self"—the stakeholder is yourself
  • Week structure: Prep activities, assignments, in-class, peer conversations
  • Productive Reflection: "10 min thinking BEFORE any AI interaction"
  • Capability badges: Which capabilities each assignment develops
  • Scaffolding: Sprint 1 has most support; Sprint 4 has most autonomy

The Scaffolding Progression

Sprint 1: Yourself as stakeholder. Sprint 2: Someone you know. Sprint 3: Limited access stakeholder. Sprint 4: Full autonomy. Challenge increases while support decreases.

Questions to consider:

  • Is this too much structure? Too little?
  • What's being graded vs. explicitly NOT graded?
  • How does this prepare students for work settings?

Human Value Statement

▼

The sprint capstone—students articulate what THEY contributed that AI couldn't.

Open Human Value Statement →

Look for:

  • The core question: "What would be worse about my solution if I had simply handed the problem to AI?"
  • The visual: "With your involvement" vs. "AI alone"
  • Weak vs. strong statement examples—what makes the difference
  • The four prompts students must address

This is HVP in Practice

Every sprint ends with this. Students don't just use AI—they're forced to articulate what they specifically brought that AI couldn't. Over 4 sprints, they build a portfolio of human value evidence.

Questions to consider:

  • Can students actually answer this well? What would help them?
  • How do you assess a Human Value Statement?
  • What would this look like in CTI programs?
