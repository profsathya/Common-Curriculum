The Framing

25-30 min (Sathya)

‚ñº

Sathya presents the conceptual framework that connects yesterday's experience to the larger challenge CTI is trying to address.

Point A ‚Üí Point B

Point A (students entering): "Here's my code, does it work?"‚Äîfocused on implementation details, asking low-level questions, dependent on specific tools and syntax.

Point B (students leaving): Able to work at the level of "what" rather than "how"‚Äîdirecting AI systems, evaluating outputs critically, knowing when to intervene and when to delegate.

The gap between A and B isn't about learning more tools. It's about developing different capabilities‚Äîcritical evaluation, problem framing, outcome orientation.

The Assumptions

  • AI capabilities will continue to increase rapidly
  • The "how" of implementation will increasingly be delegated
  • Human value shifts to the "what" and "why" and "is this right?"
  • Current curricula don't develop these capabilities systematically

The Problem We're Actually Solving

Not: "How do we teach students to use AI tools?"

But: "How do we develop the capabilities that matter when implementation is no longer the bottleneck?"

This reframing changes everything‚Äîwhat we teach, how we teach it, and how we know if it's working.

PhD students often arrive technically excellent but struggle with the transition from "solve assigned problems" to "identify which problems matter." The same pattern is emerging earlier‚Äîstudents need to develop problem-finding and outcome-orientation skills that traditional curricula assume will emerge naturally.

The capabilities we're trying to develop aren't new‚Äîthey're the ones that have always distinguished exceptional practitioners. What's new is that they're becoming essential much earlier in the learning journey.

Enter CST395

CST395 is an attempt to design a course that systematically develops these capabilities. You're about to explore it‚Äînot to validate it, but to understand what it's trying to do and surface what's missing or unclear.
