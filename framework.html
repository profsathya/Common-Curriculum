The AI-Native Professional Framework

for human work in an AI-augmented world

This framework names what it takes to create human value in an AI-augmented world. Two outcomes you aim for, three durable capabilities you build to produce them, one foundational practice that develops the capabilities, and the conversations and habits of mind the practice rests on. Each card below opens for the details.

Outcomes

Superagency

“What problems are now within my reach that I would not have attempted before?”

Superagency is the expansion of what you can attempt. With AI as a thinking partner, problems that would have been out of reach become tractable — and you pursue them with confidence in your own judgment, not deference to the tool.

Human Value Proposition

“What would be worse about my solutions if I had simply handed the problems to AI?”

Your Human Value Proposition is what you specifically bring to a solution that AI alone could not — the judgment, context, intersection, and restraint that make the outcome better than a polished AI default.

Capabilities

SDL

Self-Directed Learner

A meta-learning architecture for rapid expertise

What It Means

Self-Directed Learners develop a meta-learning architecture — a systematic approach to acquiring expertise in unfamiliar domains rapidly. This capability requires more than desire to learn; it’s knowing what you need to learn versus what you can strategically ignore, and using AI as learning infrastructure to build the required just-in-time depth.

Example in Action

When a builder starts work on patient communication tools, they need to understand medical terminology, HIPAA privacy regulations, clinical workflows, patient psychology under stress, and how different age groups interact with technology — in days or weeks, not semesters.

How we think about developing this in learners

Self-directed learning is treated as a muscle that strengthens through deliberate, low-stakes practice. Learners reflect by hand before consulting AI — slowing the loop down so the question “what do I actually need to learn here?” gets asked. The territory expands progressively: from learning about oneself, to learning about a partner’s domain, to learning from indirect sources when no expert is at hand. The end state: AI used as learning infrastructure, not as an answer machine, to build just-in-time depth.

IS

Integrative Solver

Operating at intersections where human value concentrates

What It Means

Integrative Solvers apply self-directed learning capacity across domains, developing T-shaped (or even m-shaped) expertise — deep knowledge in one technical area combined with broad boundary-crossing competencies. In the AI age, the horizontal bar becomes critical: they operate at intersections between systems of knowledge and multiple stakeholders’ perspectives where AI struggles but human value concentrates.

Example in Action

When analyzing customer complaints, an Integrative Solver doesn’t just build the requested sentiment analysis dashboard. They spend time with the complaint handlers, discovering that 50 patients generate 60% of complaints — but nobody realized this because complaints were filed by date, not patient. They bridge from technical capability to human workflow, delivering simple frequency analysis that creates more value than a sophisticated tool.

How we think about developing this in learners

Learners operate across boundaries from the start. Structured conversations with people — not just systems — build the muscle of asking, listening, and finding the real problem. The distinction between observation and interpretation is treated as a named cognitive move, not an unspoken assumption. Practice expands from working alongside experts, to entering someone else’s domain, to working when no expert is at hand. Throughout, the question stays the same: “what did you specifically contribute that AI couldn’t?”

AB

Adaptive Builder

Executing through cycles of build, test, and adapt

What It Means

Adaptive Builders execute under uncertainty — when full information isn’t available, when the right answer can’t be reasoned out in advance. They synthesize self-directed learning and integrative solving through cycles of building, testing, learning from failures, and adapting. They exercise restraint in complexity, discovering through iteration that simple solutions executed well often deliver more value than sophisticated ones.

Example in Action

An Adaptive Builder starts with basic frequency analysis rather than ambitious sentiment analysis, testing whether it solves the core problem. Through iteration, they learn to meet user needs without over-engineering. The cycle teaches them to distinguish symptoms from root causes and to iterate toward value rather than complexity.

How we think about developing this in learners

Iteration is scaffolded explicitly, then the scaffold is withdrawn. Learners get a shared vocabulary for the build process and move through build → review → revise → rebuild cycles — under uncertainty, not as polish. Reasoning is documented at the time of decision; demonstrations require working evidence, not plans. The pedagogical bet: learners learn to iterate well when they can’t reason their way to the answer in advance.

Foundational Practice

🧠 Symbiotic Thinking

Working with AI and people as cognitive partners — not tools to delegate to, not oracles to defer to

Symbiotic Thinking is the foundational practice this framework is built on: a human-led pursuit of better ideas than any party — you, an AI, or a peer — could reach alone. You stay in the driver’s seat. AI and people are thinking partners you push back on, contribute to, and converge with.

The conversations below — synchronous (listening, talking) and asynchronous (reading, writing) — are how Symbiotic Thinking is expressed. The habits of mind (slow down, know yourself, take the lead) are the dispositions it demands. Practicing it is what develops the three capabilities above — and the capabilities are what produce Superagency and the Human Value Proposition.

How we think about developing this in learners

Symbiotic Thinking is treated as something practiced, not lectured. Learners are put into AI and peer conversations with a stake — a problem they own, a decision they have to defend. Handwritten reflection precedes prompting; structured peer conversations precede solo conclusions. Over time, learners learn to push back, contribute, and converge with cognitive partners rather than accept first answers — and to name when they did it well and when they didn’t.

The process for cultivating these durable capabilities

Conversations: how thinking is expressed Synchronous (Listening · Talking) · Asynchronous (Reading · Writing)

Thinking isn’t invisible — it shows up in how we listen, talk, read, and write. The framework exercises all four.

Synchronous — thinking expressed in the moment

👂 Listening

Understanding others’ perspectives. Asking clarifying questions, receiving feedback, finding the real problem.

Exercised through: Peer conversations, stakeholder interviews, thought partnering

🗣️ Talking

Explaining ideas out loud. Defending positions, articulating value, making the implicit explicit.

Exercised through: Peer conversations, check-ins, presentations

Asynchronous — thinking expressed across time

📖 Reading

Extracting meaning from structured information. Recognizing patterns, following arguments, identifying what matters.

Exercised through: Course content, documentation, AI outputs

✍️ Writing

Articulating your thinking clearly. Structuring arguments, explaining decisions, documenting reasoning.

Exercised through: Reflection notebooks, goal setting, demonstrations

Habits of Mind: what the AI age demands Slow Down · Know Yourself · Take the Lead

AI makes speed easy. These habits make speed valuable.

⏸️

Slow Down

Resist the urge to immediately generate. Pause before prompting. Think before asking AI to think.

Built through: Handwritten reflections, wait time, deliberate process

🪞

Know Yourself

Understand your patterns, gaps, and growth edges. Self-awareness enables self-direction.

Built through: Reflection practice, peer feedback, evidence collection

🎯

Take the Lead

You direct the work. AI assists. Maintain agency over goals, quality standards, and decisions.

Built through: Self-set goals, autonomous projects, defensible portfolios

How It All Comes TogetherDeep conversations and habits of mind grow three capabilities that work as one loop

The three capabilities work as one loop, with Symbiotic Thinking at the center: you learn what the problem actually needs (SDL), connect what you learn to the people and domains involved (IS), and build, test, and adapt (AB). Each round of building reveals the next thing to learn.

The ability to have deep conversations and the habits of mind are foundational, applying across all three capabilities — a reliable base that helps individuals own the core processes of their life, at work and beyond, to reach their potential.
