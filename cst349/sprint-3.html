Sprint 3: Future

"Where is this going?" — Reading the future of your field with honest eyes

Weeks 9–12 Low Scaffolding SDL Focus: Evaluation, Collaboration
Why this sprint?

Every person in tech will spend their career navigating change faster than any course can cover. The professionals who stay relevant develop a practice for reading the environment, finding signals that matter, and making well-reasoned claims about where things are going.

Sprint 3 is about futures thinking — not predicting the future, but building the skill of reading it. You'll audit what you consume, separate signals from noise, and make a specific, defensible claim about the near future of your field.

The claim you make in Sprint 3 becomes the input for Sprint 4, where you develop one skill to prepare for that future. A vague claim gives Sprint 4 nothing to build on.

By Sprint End, You Will Have:

  • Audited your actual information diet — what you consume vs. what you think you consume
  • Learned to distinguish signals (indicators of directional change) from noise (just news)
  • Identified drivers — the underlying forces that make signals meaningful
  • Made a specific, falsifiable claim about the near future of a field connected to what you care about
  • Presented a Futures Brief showing your thinking arc, not just your conclusion
Sprint 3 Grading: Goals 20% (individual) · Reflections 20% (individual) · Demo 40% (individual) · Engagement 20%

Note: Reflections are individual this sprint — no group averaging. Sprint 3 is a transfer test.

Optional resource: Coursera Futures Thinking Specialization. Not required — everything needed will be covered in class. One quick check-in around Week 10.

Futures Brief Presentations

Format (4–5 min):

  1. Focus area — what intersection of tech + what you care about you've been tracking
  2. Three signals — specific, sourced, from at least 2 domains
  3. The driver — the underlying force connecting them
  4. Your claim — one specific, falsifiable statement about your field in 2–3 years
  5. What would change your mind — one honest acknowledgment of disconfirming evidence
