Key Concepts & Frameworks

The mental models that guide your learning journey

Meta-Habits

Slow Down

The foundation of all learning

Your brain needs time to process. Handwritten reflections enforce this habit by removing the temptation to type fast or use AI. When you slow down, you notice things you'd otherwise miss — confusion, assumptions, connections. The notebook isn't busywork; it's training your brain to think before acting.

Know Yourself

Metacognition in practice

What are your patterns? When do you avoid hard things? When do you overcommit? What triggers your defensiveness? Self-knowledge isn't navel-gazing — it's the data you need to improve. You can't fix what you don't see.

Take the Lead

Agency over your learning

Nobody will care more about your development than you. Taking the lead means not waiting for someone to tell you what to learn, not blaming circumstances for gaps, and owning the outcome of your learning regardless of external factors.

Frameworks

UMPIRE Framework

A structured approach to problem-solving

A systematic way to approach any learning or problem-solving challenge:

Understand
Map/Match
Plan
Implement
Reflect
Evaluate

3Cs: Context, Choices, Confirmation

Framework for every AI interaction

When working with AI, always provide: Context (what you're trying to accomplish and why), Choices (what options you're considering), and seek Confirmation (verify the AI's response makes sense before acting). This prevents you from offloading your thinking to AI.

5 Whys

Getting to root causes

When you identify a gap or problem, ask "why" five times. The first answer is rarely the real barrier. Each "why" peels back another layer until you find the root cause. This prevents you from solving symptoms while the real problem persists.

Practices

Productive Reflections

Learning about your learning

Not journaling. Not venting. Productive reflections answer specific questions about your learning process: What worked? What didn't? What did you notice? What will you do differently? The goal is metacognitive awareness — understanding how you learn so you can learn better.

Peer Conversations (Interview Practice)

Learning through structured dialogue

Each sprint's peer conversations develop specific interview-relevant skills. Sprint 1: answering questions thoughtfully. Sprint 2: asking good questions. Sprint 3: explaining technical concepts. Sprint 4: defending your work. These aren't casual chats — they're deliberate practice.

Evidence Collection

Building your portfolio case

From day one, you're collecting evidence for your final defense. Every reflection, every assignment, every peer conversation is potential evidence of SDL capability. The question isn't "did I complete this?" but "what does this prove about my ability to learn?"

Interview Skill Progression

1
Answer Questions Thoughtfully
Behavioral interview practice: "Tell me about a time when..."
2
Ask Good Questions
Informational interview skills: probe for specifics, challenge without hostility
3
Explain Technical Concepts
Technical interview practice: adapt explanations when first attempt doesn't land
4
Defend Your Work
Portfolio defense: support claims with evidence, acknowledge weaknesses honestly
