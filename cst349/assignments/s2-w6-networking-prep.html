Networking Event Preparation

Prepare for the alumni networking event ¬∑ Due Monday, March 2

The networking event is an opportunity to practice professional conversation AND gather external evidence of your growth. The people you meet can validate (or challenge) your self-assessment.

Preparation is the difference between a productive conversation and an awkward one.

Your Preparation Plan

1

Your elevator pitch

In 30 seconds, how would you describe the skill you're developing and why it matters? Write it out and practice saying it aloud.

2

Questions to ask

Draft 3-5 questions you want to ask alumni. Focus on questions that help you understand how this skill plays out in professional settings.

3

Evidence gathering plan

How will this event connect to your demo? What specific feedback or insights would count as evidence for Slide 4 (evidence of growth)?

4

Follow-up plan

How will you follow up after the event? A thank-you message? A LinkedIn connection? Plan it now so you actually do it.

Fictional example for the "active listening" student. Adapt the approach for YOUR skill.

‚ùå Generic question: "Is listening important in your job?"
Problem: Everyone says yes. You learn nothing. You can't be wrong.

‚úÖ Assumption-testing question: "I've been assuming that active listening mostly means not interrupting ‚Äî just letting people finish. In your experience, is that what actually distinguishes a good listener from a bad one at work, or is it something different?"
Why this works: It states a specific belief that could be WRONG. The professional's answer either confirms or breaks your assumption. Either way, you leave with real data.
Elevator pitch example: "I'm a junior CS major and I've been working on developing active listening ‚Äî specifically the gap between hearing someone and actually making them feel understood. I realized in a group project that I was waiting for my turn to talk instead of processing what people said. So I'm curious how listening plays out in your work."

Notice: the pitch includes a specific, honest gap ("waiting for my turn to talk") ‚Äî not a polished strength. Professionals respond better to honesty about a real struggle than to rehearsed confidence. You're not selling yourself here; you're testing your assumptions.

The networking event is next week. When a professional asks "what are you working on?" ‚Äî do you have a real answer with real progress, or just a plan? If you haven't started practicing yet, this week is the time. You don't need to show mastery at the event. You need to show you've actually started and have something honest to talk about.

Networking Tips

  • Ask open-ended questions, then listen more than you talk
  • Take notes immediately after conversations (not during)
  • Connect what you hear to your Sprint 2 goals
  • It's OK to say "I'm working on developing [skill] ‚Äî what's your experience with that?"
