Sprint 3 Goal Setting SDL

📋 Assignment · Individual · Due

What This Is

Sprint 3 is different from the first two sprints. You're not building a skill (Sprint 1) or demonstrating growth to a group (Sprint 2). You're learning to read the future of a space you care about — and by Week 12, you'll make a specific, defensible claim about where it's going.

That requires raw material. You can't make a claim about the future if you're not consuming anything beyond what the algorithm feeds you. This goal-setting assignment asks you to commit to three things: a focus area, an intake plan, and three questions you'll answer by the end of the sprint.

Part 1: What Do You Care About?

Pick a focus area — what you care about or want to be working on for the next 5–10 years. This doesn't have to be a job title. It's the intersection of technology/computing and something that matters to you. A vague focus area produces vague signals. A specific one gives you something to build on.

🛠 Building tools that help people solve everyday problems
🌍 Making technology accessible to underserved communities
🤖 Working at the cutting edge of AI and machine learning
🔒 Protecting people, systems, and data (cybersecurity / privacy)
📊 Using data to help organizations make better decisions
🎮 Creating games, interactive media, or immersive experiences
⚙ Building and scaling the infrastructure behind software systems
🏥 Applying tech in a specific sector: healthcare, education, public service, agriculture, finance, etc.
✏ Something else — write your own. Be specific about what you care about and how computing connects to it.

Why this matters for Sprint 4

The claim you make in Sprint 3 about this focus area becomes the input for Sprint 4, where you'll develop one skill to prepare for the future you identified. Pick something you actually care about — not something that sounds impressive. Students who are honest here have a real Sprint 4. Students who perform have nothing.

Part 2: Your Intake Commitment

To make a specific claim about the future of your focus area, you need to actually be reading and listening — beyond your normal feed. Set your own targets for the sprint. Be realistic but push yourself beyond your current habits. Ranges provided so you know what's reasonable. You choose your number.

Articles or reports I will read across the sprint typical: 6–12
Podcasts or video talks I will listen to / watch typical: 2–5
Different sources I will use (not all from the same place) typical: 4–8
Sources I've never used before minimum: 2

This is a commitment to yourself, not a compliance exercise. At the end of the sprint, you'll self-assess against these targets in your bridge reflection. Did you meet them? Exceed them? Fall short? The honest answer matters more than the number.

Where to look: trade publications for your focus area, IEEE Spectrum, MIT Technology Review, Ars Technica, The Verge, industry-specific podcasts, research lab blogs, professional association publications, Coursera's Futures Thinking specialization, reports from WEF or McKinsey, newsletters from people working in your space. Ask peers and instructor during Week 9 class.

Part 3: Three Questions You'll Answer by Sprint End

These are the questions your entire sprint builds toward. You won't have good answers yet — that's fine. Write your honest first attempt now. Your Futures Brief in Week 12 is your final, evidence-backed answer.

1

What do I care about or want to be working on for the next 5–10 years — and what do I actually know vs. assume about what that space looks like right now?

Be honest about where your knowledge ends and your assumptions begin. That gap is exactly where your signal-finding should focus.

2

What's changing in that space right now that could reshape it in 2–3 years?

Not "AI will change everything." What specifically? What signals have you seen, and what driver is behind them? You may not know yet — name what you've noticed so far.

3

What would I need to be able to do that I can't do now, if those changes play out?

This is a first guess. It becomes the foundation of Sprint 4. Don't overthink it — name the gap as you see it today.

What to Submit

SDL 1: Goals SDL 2: Planning

Your submission should include:

  1. Focus area — which option you chose (or your own), plus 2–3 sentences on why this is what you care about, not just what sounds good.
  2. Intake targets — your specific numbers for articles, podcasts, sources, and new sources. These are your targets — own them.
  3. First-attempt answers to the three questions above. One paragraph each. These don't need to be polished — they need to be honest. You'll compare these to your final Futures Brief in Week 12.
Grading: Goal quality and specificity, not "right answers." A goal-setting assignment that says "I'll read some articles about AI" is worth less than one that says "I'll read 8 articles from at least 5 sources about how AI is changing clinical decision-making in healthcare, including 2 sources I've never used."
