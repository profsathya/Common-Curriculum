How this works · Read first

The Look-back workflow — four steps.

  1. Open your existing "Sprint 4 Look-backs" Google Doc. Add a new section titled "Look-back 3 — Networking Event".
  2. Open both originals in new tabs and re-read them before you write: the Networking Event Prep and the Networking Event Report. The gap between the two is often where the answer is.
  3. Write your responses to the 6 prompts below, directly in your Look-back doc. Strong/weak examples are there to calibrate — not to copy.
  4. Export as PDF and submit here on Canvas. File → Download → PDF Document. Each submission is the full Look-back doc to date.

The 6 prompts

Step 1 of 6

What surprised you re-reading the Prep + Report?

One observation from the documents — not a feeling about how far you've come.

Strong

"My Prep questions were almost all about how to get a job, not about whether the job I was assuming I wanted was actually a good fit. The Report has nothing about that gap."

Names something specific that the documents reveal — not a feeling, an observation.

Weak

"I was surprised how much I learned at the event."

Doesn't engage with what you actually wrote. "Learned a lot" is what people say when they haven't reread anything.

Step 2 of 6

What was the Networking Event assignment trying to develop in you?

Not "networking." Name the actual capability underneath the activity.

Strong

"It was developing the ability to bring assumptions into a conversation deliberately — name what I think is true about the industry, then test it against people who actually work in it."

Names a specific skill (assumption-testing) and the mechanism (deliberate framing).

Weak

"It was about networking and making connections."

That's the literal title. The assignment was developing something underneath the activity.

Step 3 of 6

What would a genuine attempt have looked like for you, specifically?

Specific to your situation, your industry, your assumptions.

Strong

"I'd have walked in with three specific things I believed about working in cybersecurity that I wasn't sure were true. After each conversation I'd have crossed one off as confirmed, modified, or wrong. My actual Report has none of that — it has 'I learned a lot about the industry.'"

Describes a specific test structure that didn't happen and points at the document evidence.

Weak

"I should have asked better questions and taken more notes."

Vague improvement language. "Better" isn't a direction.

Step 4 of 6

The wrong way — and whether you took it.

What was the wrong way to approach this assignment, and did you do it that way?

Strong

"The wrong way was to treat it as a social exercise where the goal was to come back with quotes I could put in a report. I did it that way. I picked the most quotable answers, not the answers that contradicted what I came in believing."

Names a specific tradeoff (quotability over contradiction) made under pressure.

Weak

"The wrong way is to not network or not reflect on it."

Identifies a way of failing that wasn't actually available to you (you did the assignment).

Step 5 of 6

What you actually gained (or would have gained).

If you treated the event as a real assumption test, what changed in how you think about your field? If you didn't, what did the workaround cost you?

Step 6 of 6

One sentence about how you actually use other people to test your thinking.

Across the Networking Event and the rest of Sprint 2. The honest sentence about whether you bring your real questions to other people, or whether you bring polished ones.
