Conversation Technique

How to Ask Questions
That Go Deeper

When someone shares their skill choice, your job is to help them get from the label to the real thing underneath.

1

Push past the label

The first answer is always broad. "Communication," "time management," "leadership." That's a category, not a skill.

"What about communication specifically? Like, what goes wrong?"

2

Ask for a specific moment

Don't ask what they think. Ask what happened. A real story from Sprint 1 or their life.

"Was there a moment in Sprint 1 where this was actually the problem?"

3

Test if it's real or borrowed

Many skill choices come from "what everyone says you need" ‚Äî not from actual experience. Surface that.

"Did something actually happen that showed you this, or is it more of a general feeling?"

4

Name the real skill

Reflect back what you're hearing. "So it's not X in general ‚Äî it's specifically Y?" Let them confirm or correct.

"So it's not communication ‚Äî it's making your thinking visible to people who didn't watch you build it?"

Sample Conversation 1

"I want to work on communication"

A I picked communication because I know I need to get better at it for interviews.
B What about communication specifically? Like, can you talk to people fine in normal life?

Push past the label. "Communication" is a category, not a skill.

A Yeah, I'm fine with friends. It's more like professional settings.
B What happened in Sprint 1 that made you think of this?

Ask for a specific moment. Not what they think ‚Äî what happened.

A I don't know, it's just something everyone says you need.
B But did something actually happen? Like, was there a moment where communication was the problem?

Test if it's real or borrowed. Don't let "everyone says" stand.

A ...I guess during the demo. I knew my project was good but when I was presenting I couldn't explain why my design decisions made sense. People just saw the output.
B So it's not communication in general ‚Äî it's explaining your reasoning? Like, making your thinking visible to people who didn't watch you build it?

Name the real skill.

A Yeah. I can build things but I can't show people the thinking behind it.

Surface skill: "Communication"
Real skill: Articulating design rationale ‚Äî making invisible thinking visible to an audience. That's specific, evidence-based, and developable in 4 weeks. "Communication" isn't.

Sample Conversation 2

"I want to learn Docker"

A I want to learn Docker because it keeps coming up in job postings.
B OK, but this sprint is about skills beyond technical. What would Docker give you that you don't have?

Push past the label. Redirect from the tool to the purpose.

A I mean, it's a skill employers want.
B Do you know that from actual job applications you've looked at, or is it just what people say?

Test if it's real or borrowed. Same move, different context.

A ...I guess I've seen it mentioned. I haven't really looked at specific jobs.
B What if you looked at 5 actual postings for jobs you'd want and saw what they really ask for? Would Docker even be on there?
A Honestly, I don't even know what kind of job I want yet. That might be the bigger problem.

They named it themselves. The real gap surfaced.

Surface skill: "Docker" (technical checkbox)
Real skill gap: Hasn't examined what direction they're heading ‚Äî picking tools before picking a destination. The SDL skill isn't Docker. It's learning how to evaluate what's actually worth learning.

What to notice

The Pattern

Both conversations followed the same arc:

  1. The first answer was a label, not a skill ‚Äî "communication," "Docker"
  2. When asked for evidence, they couldn't point to a specific moment
  3. The real skill was hiding underneath ‚Äî and it surprised them
  4. The partner didn't lecture. They asked questions until the person found it themselves

Your group's job isn't to judge each other's skill choices. It's to ask questions until the real skill shows up.
