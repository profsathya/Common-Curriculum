Why SDL Direction Matters

What you choose to learn is shaped by three things.

The overlap is where work feels like yours. Sprint 3 helps you find where the world needs are shifting — so you can aim for the overlap, not just the intersection you happened to land in.

The Targeting Problem

Shooting at the flock vs. aiming at one bird.

Volume strategy

Finding A Job

Random applications. Any open role. Skills that match a job description. Hoping to get noticed. This works when there are a lot of birds in the sky.

Direction strategy

Finding THE Job

Strategic focus. You know which field is moving, which role is emerging, which skill is becoming rare. You're building toward a specific intersection.

The professionals who stay relevant aren't the ones who learned the most. They're the ones who noticed where the ground was shifting — before it shifted under them. That's what Sprint 3 builds.

Two Key Concepts

Signals and Drivers

Signal

A specific, observable change that indicates something bigger is happening. Not a prediction — something you can point to that already exists.

Example

Retail, ~2013: Malls start reporting declining foot traffic even as consumer spending increases.

Driver

The underlying force that makes a signal meaningful. Drivers explain why something is happening — and whether it will keep happening.

Example

Driver: Smartphone adoption made comparison shopping frictionless — physical stores lost their information advantage.

A signal without a driver is just news. A driver without a signal is just a theory. You need both.

The Activity · Why 2010–2020

You already lived through a decade of signals. You just didn't call them that.

  • We're looking at 2010–2020 — not AI, not the last 2 years
  • Many of you were 6–14 years old during this decade. You didn't observe it as analysts. But it happened around you — in your family's work, in your neighborhood, in what disappeared and what appeared.
  • Your job: find one signal from that decade in a domain you've been assigned. Name the driver underneath it.

Two tools available — your choice:

AI Prompt (starter)

I'm looking at [your domain] between 2010 and 2020. Describe three specific things that changed in that industry during that decade, from the perspective of someone who worked in it. For each change: what was a signal that it was coming before it happened, and what underlying force was driving it?

Round 1 · Individual · 5 min

One signal. Notebook first.

  • Choose your domain from your group's three options
  • Use the AI prompt, Google search, or signal cards doc to find one specific change in that domain between 2010–2020
  • Write in your notebook:

    The signal — what specifically changed, and approximately when it became visible

    The driver — the underlying force that made it happen

The test for a good signal: Could someone working in that industry have seen this coming if they were paying attention? What would they have noticed first?

5:00

Round 2 · Groups of 3 · 5 min

Same domain, same decade — did you find the same thing?

Form groups of 3 within your group — pick people who chose the same domain:

  • Share your signal
  • Where do you agree on the driver? Where do you disagree?
  • The disagreement is more interesting than the agreement — write it down
One question to push each other

"What would have to be true for that NOT to have happened?" — that's how you test a driver.

5:00

Round 3 · Your Group · 10 min

Pick the one signal your group is most willing to defend.

Pool all signals from your group members. Then:

  • Pick the one that's most defensible — not most dramatic, most defensible
  • Agree on one driver statement in one sentence
  • Prepare to present: signal (what + when), driver (why it was inevitable once it started)

The constraint: you have to be able to say "Even if [something else] hadn't happened, this signal would still have appeared because of [driver]."

10:00

What We Found · Class Discussion · 15 min

Six domains, one decade. What do the drivers have in common?

Each group: 60 seconds. Signal + driver. No explanation of how you found it.

After all groups share, two questions:

1. "What driver kept appearing across different domains under different names?"

2. "If you were 25 years old in 2012 and paying close attention — what would you have done differently to prepare for 2020?"

That second question is Sprint 3. You just described the practice of futures thinking using a decade you can evaluate in hindsight. Now you build that practice for the next decade.

Interview Practice · Sprint 3

Explaining what you found to someone who wasn't looking.

This sprint's skill

How to explain technical concepts — specifically, how to describe a signal or trend to someone who doesn't already share your context.

Today's prompt

"Walk your partner through the signal you found. Their job: ask the one question that most challenges your driver. Your job: answer it without retreating to 'it's complicated.'"

  • Pairing: you'll be partnered with someone from a different group — someone who worked on a different domain. They haven't seen your signal. That's the point.
  • Skill being practiced: making a claim and defending it under skeptical questioning from someone with no prior context — which is every technical interview.
  • No submission.
