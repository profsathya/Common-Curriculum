Looking Back · Networking Event

What the Professionals Told You

22 of you submitted a report. Here's what came through.

🤖

AI is a tool, not a threat — if you know how to use it

Almost every professional brought up AI unprompted. Senior engineers use it heavily. The ones who thrive know when to push back on it.

"Become AI native"  ·  "Be suspicious. Read it."

💬

Communication matters as much as code

Nearly every student said this surprised them. Technical skill is assumed. What distinguishes you is how you think out loud and work with others.

"Most programming is done with others — the better you communicate, the better it helps you."

🎯

Initiative is what gets you noticed

Class projects get you in the door. What you built independently, the questions you asked, the problems you chased — that's what makes you memorable.

"We don't just hire people who can code — we hire people who can think."

🔄

Your assumptions were wrong in useful ways

Students expected AI skepticism, technical skill to dominate, credentials to matter most. Every one of those assumptions got challenged.

"10% skill, 90% personality"

Structure

How Today Works

The Format

  • You'll present in combined groups of 5–7 (two groups together)
  • Each person gets 4–6 minutes to present
  • Then 2–3 minutes of questions from the group
  • While someone presents, everyone else is taking live notes using the Peer Observation tool
📋

Open the Peer Observation tool now — it's in the Sprint 2 assignments. Use the three lenses (Notice / Interpret / Ask) while each person presents. The "Push my thinking" button is there when your notes get thin.

The questions you ask after each demo are part of your communication skills practice — not a courtesy. Use the ASK lens: what question would actually test their evidence?

Demo Focus

What You're Presenting

Five slides. Show the trajectory — not just what you did, but what changed your thinking.

1

The quality & why it matters

Name the specific quality you developed. Why does it matter — not in general, but to you and your career?

2

Your starting point

Where were you at the beginning of Sprint 2? Be honest about your baseline. Use the evidence you designed in Week 5.

3

What you did

Specific actions — not just "I practiced." What exactly, when, how often? What did your group help with? What did you try that didn't work?

4

Evidence of growth

Before/after comparisons, external feedback, concrete examples with dates. What would convince a skeptic? Is this evidence of growth — or just effort?

5

Your trajectory / what's next

Honest assessment of where you are and where you're going. What will you continue working on? What did this sprint teach you about how you grow?

The strongest demos aren't the most polished. They're the most honest.
