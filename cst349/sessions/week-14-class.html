Future of Work

What’s actually happening

The rules of work are about to change. Again. And again.

LinkedIn

More CS grads than ever. Lowest entry-level software hiring in years. Same chart.

World Economic Forum

39% of the skills you need will be different by 2030.

Stanford · Brynjolfsson et al.

Hiring of 22–25-year-olds in AI-exposed jobs is down ~14%.

Three reports. They partially disagree. The signal they share: whatever job you pick, the rules will keep changing.

What survives

The data agrees on what wins.

LinkedIn · Skills on the Rise 2026

7 of the top 10 fastest-growing skills are human-centric — adaptability, conflict mitigation, public speaking, strategic thinking, innovative thinking, process optimization, solution-based selling. AI Literacy is #1, but it sits alongside the others.

WEF · Future of Jobs

Resilience, flexibility, agility, curiosity and lifelong learning are among the top growing skill clusters through 2030.

McKinsey

The shift in work is from doing the writing and analysis to directing and judging the writing and analysis.

Deloitte

70% of business leaders say their primary competitive strategy for the next 3 years is being “fast and nimble.” Speed at the team level requires people who can absorb feedback and shift.

Anthropic · 81,000-user survey

Experienced AI users treat AI as a collaborator. Newcomers hand work to it. The first group is measurably more productive.

Your defense

The defense is asking one thing: do you know how to grow?

It’s not a status report. It’s evidence — across one semester — that you can spot a real gap, name it cleanly, and close it on purpose.

Today is the rehearsal. We’re going to look at two real Gap Statement drafts from this class and pressure-test them out loud, the way your defense panel will.

Pair up. The questions on the next two slides stay on screen — work through them together.

From this class · 1 of 2

Pressure-test this gap.

Anonymized. A real gap statement from this cohort.

“I struggle to recognize in real time when I shift from genuinely understanding concepts to simply completing tasks through copying patterns or surface-level problem solving.”

1

McKinsey: the shift in work is from doing the writing and analysis to directing and judging it. The skill named here — recognizing the shift in real time — IS that judgment skill. What’s the earliest signal that tells you you’ve slipped from understanding to pattern-matching?

2

Anthropic’s 81,000-user survey: experienced users treat AI as a collaborator. Newcomers hand work to it. That difference is what this gap is pointing at. What would a learning log look like that captured these moments specifically — not “what I did” but “where I caught myself”?

3

Brynjolfsson’s research: what AI struggles to replicate is tacit knowledge — the in-the-moment sense that something’s off. This gap targets tacit knowledge directly. How would you know in 2 weeks that this skill is sharper, given that it’s invisible by definition?

From this class · 2 of 2

Pressure-test this one.

Different terrain. Same underlying move.

“Receiving feedback — I can give feedback but I tend to become rigid when receiving feedback and have a hard time incorporating it into my work.”

1

Deloitte: 70% of business leaders say their top strategy for the next 3 years is being “fast and nimble.” Team speed depends on people who absorb feedback and shift, instead of absorbing feedback and defending. What does “incorporating feedback” look like as observable behavior — in the next 2 weeks, in a real project?

2

WEF lists “resilience, flexibility, agility” as a top growing skill cluster through 2030. The hard part is that rigidity feels like principled disagreement from the inside. How will you tell, in the moment, whether you’re disagreeing for a reason or defending out of habit?

3

The most honest line in this gap statement is that the writer hasn’t named this before — because naming it invites the exact thing they’re avoiding. The vehicle has to break that loop. What’s a structure where the cost of refusing feedback is higher than the cost of taking it?

Going forward

The whole course in one line.

Spot the gap. Close it. Find the next one.

This semester was rep four. Rep five comes next semester. Rep six the semester after. A hundred more after graduation. The rules of work will keep changing — that’s the only thing they do. The reps don’t stop. The skill is the practice.
