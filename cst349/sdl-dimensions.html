UMPIRE: How Self-Directed Learners Think

A thinking cycle, not a checklist

UMPIRE is how good learners think when they face something they don't know yet. It's not six separate assignments or six weeks of class — it's a cycle you run every time you encounter a real problem, a new domain, or a gap in your understanding.

The six phases are sequential on paper, but messy in practice. You'll often loop back from Plan to Map when you realize you missed something, or from Review back to Understand when the problem turns out to be different than you thought. That's the cycle working, not failing.

The Cycle

U — Understand → M — Map → P — Plan → I — Implement → R — Review → E — Evaluate

← then back to Understand, with what you now know →

U

Understand

"What's the real problem, question, or gap?"

Before you can learn anything, you have to name what you don't know — specifically, not vaguely. "I need to get better at coding" isn't understanding. "I can't write a recursive function without looking at examples every time" is. This phase is about honesty: separating what you actually know from what you assume you know.

M

Map

"What do I already know? What don't I know? Where are the resources?"

Mapping is the phase most people skip — and it's the one that determines whether everything after it works. Before you make a plan, you need to survey the territory: what's in your head already, what's out there to find, what sources are credible, what's noise. Skipping Map means you plan from assumptions instead of from evidence.

Why this phase matters most: Students consistently jump from "I have a problem" straight to "I'll Google it and start working." The Map phase is the intervention — the moment where you slow down, take stock of what you actually know vs. what you're guessing, and figure out where to look before you start doing. Every sprint in this course will push you to practice this explicitly.
P

Plan

"What's my approach? What will I try first?"

A plan doesn't need to be perfect — it needs to be specific enough to act on and honest enough to abandon when it stops working. This phase is about breaking a learning challenge into steps, choosing your first move, and deciding what "good enough" looks like before you start.

I

Implement

"Do the work. Follow the plan — and notice when you deviate."

This is the phase everyone's comfortable with: actually doing the thing. But in UMPIRE, implementation comes with a metacognitive requirement. You don't just execute — you pay attention to when the plan stops fitting reality. The gap between your plan and what actually happens is where the most learning lives.

R

Review

"Did it work? What would I change?"

Review is not "I'm done, it's fine." It's the honest look back at what you actually produced versus what you intended. Did you learn what you set out to learn? Did the approach work? What failed, and why? Review requires you to distinguish between activity (I did stuff) and progress (I actually moved forward).

E

Evaluate

"What did I learn about my learning? How does this change my approach next time?"

The phase that separates people who get better from people who just get busier. Evaluate isn't about this problem — it's about the next one. What did this cycle teach you about how you learn? What will you do differently the next time you run UMPIRE? This is where metacognition becomes a habit.

Where the 8 Dimensions Went

The original 8 SDL dimensions aren't gone — they're embedded in the UMPIRE phases. Instead of tracking 8 separate skills, you practice all of them every time you run the cycle.

1. Identify Learning Goals

U — Understand

2. Design Learning Plan

M — Map + P — Plan

3. Navigate Ambiguity

M — Map (surveying unknown territory)

4. Metacognitive Awareness

Entire cycle (especially R + E)

5. Learn Through Iteration

The cycle itself (loop back)

6. Evaluate Critically

R — Review + E — Evaluate

7. Collaborate Effectively

Every phase (peers sharpen your thinking)

8. Leverage AI Effectively

Every phase (AI as thought partner, not answer machine)

UMPIRE Across the Semester

Each sprint emphasizes different phases, but you run the whole cycle every time. By Sprint 4, the cycle should feel natural — not because we made it easy, but because you've practiced it enough.

Sprint 1 U + M + P — Identifying gaps, mapping your starting point, making a plan Sprint 2 P + I — Planning with accountability, implementing with evidence Sprint 3 M + I + R — Mapping your information landscape, implementing change, reviewing what shifted Sprint 4 I + R + E — Implementing your defense, reviewing your evidence, evaluating your growth

The Test

In your final portfolio defense, you won't walk through the UMPIRE phases like a checklist. Instead, you'll tell the story of how your thinking changed across the semester — and the evidence should make it obvious that you've been running this cycle. The question isn't "did you complete activities?" but "can you convince a skeptical audience that you can learn anything you need to learn?"
