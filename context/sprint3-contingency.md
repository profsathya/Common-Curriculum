# Sprint 3 Contingency Plan

## Core Idea

Week 9 is fully designed: collaborative prompting, problem staking, first build. Weeks 10–12 are intentionally tentative — what Week 10 becomes depends on what happens in Week 9 class (Wednesday Mar 19).

The decision gets posted publicly by **Saturday Mar 22**, including which path (or hybrid) we're taking and the specific Week 9 observations that drove the call.

---

## Scenario A — "It Landed Well"

**Signal to look for:** Strong problem stakes. Students left class with a specific human moment staked and something built (even rough). Problem Stake Briefs show genuine Bhutan context engagement — named users, specific moments, real constraints. Not domain-level claims ("making the guideline accessible") but moment-level claims ("a ward nurse needs to know X in the 4 minutes between handoffs").

**Week 10 structure — Simulation-First:**
- Students build a detailed clinical persona based on their staked problem and run their prototype against it as an AI simulation
- Goal: surface the questions only Piranavan can answer — not generic domain questions, but specific gaps in the simulation's clinical knowledge
- Students come to class with 2 specific questions for Piranavan, grounded in what the simulation couldn't resolve
- Piranavan joins in-class to respond to problem framings and tell students what the simulation gets wrong

**Assignments:**
- Simulation Dojo activity (structured AI persona simulation)
- Weekly Build Reflection #2 — what changed after the expert session
- Build v1 submission — link + one decision + one known failure mode

**Weeks 11 and 12:** Proceed as designed — Piranavan Session 2 with peer pressure test (Week 11), Demo Day with arc narrative (Week 12).

---

## Scenario B — "Operational Friction"

**Signal to look for:** Collaborative prompting or first build ran long. Problem stakes are incomplete or still at domain level ("making the guideline more accessible") rather than specific moment level. Students engaged but didn't get to the build phase, or builds are purely conceptual with nothing that exists yet.

**Week 10 structure — Regroup + Build:**
- First 20 min: accelerated re-staking. Pairs challenge each other's stakes using the strong/weak exemplar from the Problem Stake Brief assignment. Goal: every student leaves the first 20 minutes with a moment-level stake, not a domain-level one
- Piranavan joins after re-staking for domain validation — confirms whether the staked moments are real clinical moments or academic inventions
- Simulation moves to async homework (due before Week 11)
- Remaining class time: structured build session

**Note for students:** Don't wait for class — use the weekend to sharpen your stake and push the build further. If your stake is still at "improve the guideline" level, re-read the strong example in the Problem Stake Brief assignment and narrow to a person + moment + constraint.

**Weeks 11 and 12:** Week 11 compresses — simulation review + Piranavan Session 2 in the same class. Week 12 Demo Day unchanged, but demo framing shifts to emphasize the re-staking pivot as part of the arc.

---

## Scenario C — "The Design Assumption Was Wrong"

**Signal to look for:** Students engaged genuinely but the Bhutan context created confusion that stalled rather than energized. Stakes show either shallow domain engagement (surface-level parroting of the guideline) or over-literal reading with no real human moment identified. The problem isn't effort — it's that the domain distance was too large for the scaffolding provided.

This path means a design choice created a problem to solve — say that honestly.

**Week 10 structure — Domain Bridge:**
- First 30 min: structured domain immersion — three short real clinical scenarios (2–3 paragraphs each, drawn from actual MDRO situations). Students identify the decision point in each scenario. Goal: build clinical intuition through concrete examples, not abstract guideline reading
- Piranavan joins as bridge-builder, not evaluator — he narrates what actually happens in these moments, what clinicians think about, what the guideline assumes they know
- Second half: students re-stake with the new clinical context. This time the stakes should land because they've seen what real moments look like

**Note for students:** This path isn't a failure state — some of the best learning happens when the original design doesn't hold. The fact that we're adjusting is the same process you're being asked to demonstrate: build something, get signal, adjust.

**Weeks 11 and 12:** Week 11 becomes the simulation + build week (what was originally Week 10). Week 12 Demo Day proceeds but with adjusted expectations — the arc narrative becomes even more important because the pivot IS the story.

---

## Most Likely Outcome

A hybrid. Scenario A for stronger students who already showed moment-level thinking in Sprint 2. Scenario B for the middle — engaged but need the re-staking pass.

**Practical hybrid approach:** Run Scenario A class structure with a 10-minute recalibration pass at the start for students who need to sharpen their stake first. Those who are already sharp use the 10 minutes to start their simulation setup. Those who need re-staking get focused pair work during that window.

---

## The Thing to Protect

Across all scenarios: the **arc narrative for Demo Day** (Week 12). The demo isn't about the final product — it's about the delta from Week 9 to Week 12 and what changed the student's thinking along the way.

If build time gets compressed (Scenarios B or C), the demo framing shifts: delta matters more than final state. A rough build with a clear arc ("I started here, this happened, I changed direction because X, here's where I am now") is worth more than a polished product with no visible learning trajectory.
